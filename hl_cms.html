Content Review Process

Make the care plan review process more efficient to reduce time to value


HealthLoop has developed a CMS (Content Management System) for the internal clinical content team to provide patients with relevant educational materials throughout their surgery journey. This service covers a wide range of specialties and allows customizations on an individual physician basis. When there's a sales deal closed, HealthLoop team configs the drafted care plan using PDF format and sends to physicians to review before the service goes live.


The initiative started out from the company kickoff with the goal of “Make the content management process more efficient and impressive to reduce time to value.” It was an ambitious goal with a large problem space that heavily involved both external customers (physicians) and internal teams (Customer Success team and Clinical Content Writers). In the first sprint we learned a lot, but we felt that we didn’t answer a lot of the sprint questions. That's why the team had the second iteration.

DAY1: UNDERSTANDING THE PROBLEM

Sprint Questions

  • Can a new process/tool be more efficient than the existing PDF review process?
  • Can the new tool maintain/establish trust in our content?
  • Can the design reinforce HealthLoop self-invented concepts?
  • Can we better help customers understand the relationship between content and patient experience?

    Opportunities Identified

    How Might We...

  • Help customers to go through all the content efficiently?
  • Make the content review process feel like less steps?
  • Get customers to start looking at care plans and supporting documentation without feeling overwhelmed?
  • Allow reviewers to collaborate on changes before submitting the edits to our clinical content team?
  • Get customers to give high level feedback rather than small edits?
  • How might we support all reviewer types with a single tool?

    DAY5: TESTING PROTOTYPES WITH REAL USERS

    We recruited 6 doctors from different specialty's to try our prototypes remotely. We tried to get a mix of doctors who had done review and some who hadn’t. We summarized our findings in real time by writing observations on the whiteboard.




    What did we learn from the test?

    Patterns and Insights


    This tool would not likely reduce edit requests. In fact, when you place a tool like this in people’s hands they expect that they will be able to edit the care plan.

    ﹒ ﹒ ﹒


    Negative : (

  • Participants not always clear on what action to take after clicking into prototype.
  • Participants expected to be able to interact with the static component preview.
  • Half of participants didn’t know what to make of HL Certified Components especially without any explanatory text.

    • Neutral : |

  • Participants expected ability to leave comments on individual components.
  • PDF still seen as useful for focused review.
  • Participants liked the timeline visual, though couldn’t identify a use.
  • Component preview is valuable the first time it’s seen, but not after that.

    • Positive : )

  • Participants all appreciated ability to toggle organization of content.
  • Topic view made content easier to understand.
  • Day view made it easier to understand patient experience.



    • User Feedback

    It’s helpful when doctors have different protocols or preferences.

    I don’t need to go to the practice setting. It’s a good idea of having the two options of grouping things.

    I know physicians I am looking at and covering with.

    Make important things fast

    In the navigation drawer, the entry point of “Editing you provider list” was promoted one layer up from the setting so it gets better visibility.




    Minimize anxiety

    This is designed to support users who are anxious about making mistakes in the clinical handover (transition) process.


    Takeway

    User research is always invaluable to the design process

    Through talking to the users, I learned to consider the political environment and how it affects staff users’ workflow and the willingness of using certain features.


    With the new feature of sorting by doctors (physician centered), it could mean that doctor gets priority. Therefore, some clinical staff would never use it even if they believe the feature can help them to increase work efficiency. This also reveals the political environment in the healthcare organization and should be considered in the future design.



    Consider the political environment  ─  Like it ≠ Use it
